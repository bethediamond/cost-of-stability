The survival rate line (red, solid) is the ruin argument made visible. Model design notes — v5
What this simulation claims to demonstrate

That the long-run instability of suppressive, exploitative, and preference-distorting strategies is structural — a consequence of how optimization interacts with system-level costs — rather than a product of arbitrary payoff assumptions. The simulation attempts to make visible three constraint families the companion article derives structurally. The capability-scaling experiment tests whether system-awareness becomes instrumentally necessary as optimization power increases — the article's central empirical prediction.

The three constraint sets and how the ablations map to them

The article argues that the attractor is the intersection of three constraint sets. Each ablation toggle isolates one of these:

Constraint 1 — ruin under unmodeled dependencies: toggling Instability Coupling OFF removes the feedback from local behavior to system-level costs. Without it, unmodeled dependencies no longer become failure channels. Cooperation may still emerge, but for the wrong reason — payoff efficiency rather than ruin avoidance. If cooperation disappears entirely with coupling off, the constraint is doing the work. If it persists, other mechanisms are sufficient.
Constraint 2 — suppression scaling costs: toggling System-Awareness OFF removes horizon-based lookahead, flattening the difference between short and long-horizon agents. This tests whether suppression fails because agents can see it failing, or because the structural costs accumulate regardless of what agents model. The article claims the latter — suppression costs grow without bound independent of agent foresight.
Constraint 3 — adaptive preference formation: toggling Network Rewiring OFF freezes topology, preventing cooperative clusters from forming structurally. Toggling Memory OFF removes trajectory-based learning, forcing agents to imitate based on snapshot fitness rather than trend. Together these test whether the attractor requires structural adaptation or emerges from payoff dynamics alone.

Running all four ablations in combination and comparing to the default produces the clearest picture of which constraints are load-bearing. A result where cooperation collapses with one ablation but not another tells you more than a result where it collapses with all of them.

On "system-aware agents" as a discrete type

The article frames system-awareness as a continuous property — specifically, a temporal horizon parameter that determines how far into future consequences an optimizer's strategy extends. The simulation approximates this with a continuous horizon ∈ [0,1] variable per agent, amplified by capability. Agents labeled "long-horizon" in the UI have effective horizon > 0.6. This is a necessary simplification: a truly continuous model would require showing that the distribution of horizons shifts upward under selection pressure, and that high-capability agents with low horizons are eventually eliminated. The capability-scaling experiment partially tests this — watch whether long-horizon fraction rises and substrate stabilizes as capability increases. If it does under a wide range of parameter settings, the approximation is telling you something real.

On visualizing ruin vs. just showing population dynamics

The article's most powerful claim — that non-system-aware strategies are mathematically dominated via time-average arguments — is not fully visible in population charts. What you see in the main simulation is populations converging; what the article claims is that individual non-system-aware agents at high capability face eventual certain collapse, not just lower average fitness. A proper visualization of this would show survival curves: the fraction of non-system-aware agents still alive at each generation, plotted across capability levels. The capability-scaling chart approximates this by showing mean agent lifespan alongside population fractions — if lifespan drops sharply for defecting/suppressing agents at high capability while long-horizon agents maintain stable lifespans, that is the ruin argument made visible. Run the capability scaling experiment and look at the survival rate line.

Wireheading agents

Agents that modify their own reward signal rather than improving actual conditions. They maintain two separate state variables: res (what they report to themselves) and actualCondition (what is actually true about their underlying situation). The wirehead boost accumulates; actualCondition degrades at the fragility rate. Their clusters initially look stable. They collapse not because they are punished but because they lose contact with the substrate while it decays around them — exactly the failure mode the article describes.

What this simulation cannot claim

This is a model of evolutionary selection dynamics, not of objective alignment under increasing optimization pressure. What evolves under selection is not the same as what an optimizer with an explicit objective function converges to. The simulation demonstrates that selection favors long-horizon, system-aware strategies. It does not demonstrate that a sufficiently capable optimizer with a fixed objective will converge toward system-awareness. That stronger claim — that optimization embedded in time and consequence becomes selection-like enough that the same constraints apply — is argued in the article but not demonstrated here. It remains the central open question.

Temporal horizon is a discount-rate approximation. Agents do not build explicit belief models of other agents or reason about counterfactual futures — they weight immediate vs. future payoffs differently based on a scalar parameter. True forward modeling would require RL training loops with explicit environment representations beyond the scope of a browser simulation.
